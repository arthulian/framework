1

Identify recurring patterns, represent them symbolically, and map out their interconnections.

1.2

Apply consistent empirical and rational standards to all conclusions with uniform criteria of proof.

1.2.1

Approach binary thinking with nuance to prevent false dichotomies.

1.2.2

Assess propositions, their likelihood, and the robustness of supporting evidence.

1.2.3

Recognize the links between claims, evidence, and conclusions.

1.2.4

Evaluate the soundness of arguments and the relevance of evidence.

1.2.5

Absence does not equal proof of absence (except under induction-based premises).

2

Differentiate between genuine moral guilt and socially imposed shame (for violating norms).

2.2

Attribute results to controllable factors—skill, effort, strategy, conditions—rather than to character.

2.3

Balance intent against consequences; use role reversal to ensure fair judgment.

3

Actively reframe to counter distortions from cognitive anchors and framing.

3.2

Use cognitive defusion: treat internal experiences as information, not commands.

3.3

Gain confidence primarily through mastery experiences.

3.4

Base persistence on the outcomes of your actions, not on personal flaws or identity.

3.5

Confront situations you typically avoid to gradually reduce fear.

3.5.1

Build resilience by updating negative expectations when feared outcomes do not occur.

3.6

Translate values into actionable rules.

3.6.1

Consider acting on consequence-weighted "oughts."
