2.0

Epistemology: The Truth-Processing Layer

2.1

Apply consistent empirical and rational standards to all conclusions with uniform criteria of proof.

2.2

Assess propositions based on likelihood and the robustness of supporting evidence.

2.3

Recognize and validate the links between claims, evidence, and conclusions.

2.4

Evaluate the soundness of arguments and the relevance of evidence.

2.5

Avoid oversimplifications: approach thinking with nuance to prevent false dichotomies and binary thinking.

2.6

Maintain epistemological humility: Absence of evidence does not equal proof of absence (except under induction-based premises).

2.7

Actively reframe information to counter distortions from cognitive anchors and framing effects.

3.0

Ethics & Values: The Calibration Layer

3.1

Differentiate between objective guilt (factual guilt) and external blame (resulting in the feeling of shame).

3.2

Fair judgement: use role reversal to balance intent against consequence.

3.3

Operationalize values: Translate abstract moral principles into actionable rules.

3.3.1

Execute based on "consequence-weighted oughts" rather than raw impulse or dogma.

4.0

Agency & Attribution: The Performance Layer

4.1

Focus on controllable variables: Attribute results to skill, effort, strategy, and conditions rather than fixed character flaws.

4.2

Build confidence through mastery: Prioritize evidence of competence over "positive thinking."

4.3

Decouple identity from outcomes: Base persistence on the ongoing outcomes of your actions, not on personal flaws.

5.0

Psychological Flexibility: The Regulation Layer

5.1

Practice cognitive defusion: Treat internal experiences (thoughts/emotions) as data points, not commands.

5.2

Systematic Exposure: Actively confront situations you typically avoid to gradually reduce fear.

5.3

Expectancy Violation: Update negative expectations and build resilience when feared outcomes fail to occur.
