BLUEPRINT

I. EVALUATE EVIDENCE

  • Critical Thinking: Notice the plausibility of claims by tracking repeating patterns; Compare outcomes with past predictions to reassess credibility when those predictions fail; Distinguish among evidence, inference, and claims by isolating observed facts from logical leaps.
  • Sense-Making: Use counterfactual simulations (grounded in inductive reasoning) to resolve the friction between perception and intuition by retroactively altering past variables to isolate causality.

II. TEST ASSUMPTIONS

  • Falsification: Identify possible rebuttals before forming conclusions.
  • Situational Justice: Weigh the input-outcome balance of the person’s moral intent with the consequences of his actions, through contextual empathy.
  • Objectivity: Focus on factual accuracy; Refrain from self-justification or motivated rationalization; Avoid adopting double standards of verification when formating beliefs, by applying criteria uniformly.
  • The Principle of Charity: Steelman the opponent’s argument in its most committed form, in order to test whether it survives against its own commitment set.

III. SELF-COMPASSION

  • Equanimity: Practice an observer's perspective of the present moment by reframing thoughts and emotions as transient mental states, allowing you to respond rather than react.
  • Common Humanity: Frame your struggles within the context of shared humanity. Imperfection and vulnerability are qualities inherent to all human beings.
  • Active Self-Kindness: De-escalate unnecessary internal pressure through acceptance of your current limitations, taking time-outs to restore yourself to your capacity, so that recovery enables continued engagement rather than avoidance.

IV. FOUNDATIONS OF SELF-WORTH

  • Stable Self-Reference: Self-worth is derived from the volition of character to embody the transcendentals (truth, justice, love, wisdom, holiness, wholeness/undividedness, harmony/proportion, actualized potential, immutable faithfulness), which themselves are the source and standards of all true worth.
  • Defensive Self-Worth (failure mode): Avoid defensive self-reliance and performative self-sufficiency, which seek external validation.

V. OPTIMIZE STRESS

  • Distress: Under overwhelming pressure, disengage from adverse stimuli through boundary setting.
  • The Optimal Zone: Under moderate pressure, build resilience by introducing controlled, low-stakes challenges that safely test your capacity limit.

VI. UPDATE CONFIDENCE

  • Structure: Self-confidence calibrated by feedback that accurately links outcomes to efforts.
  • The loop: 1. Build skills by overcoming challenges.
    2. Credit achieved objectives to my own allotted effort, avoiding impostor syndrome by not dismissing evidence of my competence.
    3. Update my capability baseline (my confidence) based on those empirical results.
    4. Improved confidence enables tackling tougher challenges.
  • Loop guardrail: Interpret setbacks as skill deficits, not as self-worth deficits.

VII. ACT WITH PERSISTENCE

  • Conserve Mental Energy: Allocate effort to influenceable events, rather than dwelling on sunk costs. The goal is not to control your reality, but to respond intelligently within your circumstances.
  • Perseverance: Steady persistence in a course of action amidst difficulties. It overcomes fear-driven avoidance through a gradual exposure to feared stimuli, while regulating effort through active self-kindness so that persistence remains adaptive rather than degrading into burnout.
