1. INTELLECTUAL & EPISTEMIC HUMILITY
A. Core Principles
  • •Epistemic Pre-commitment:
    • ›Present disproving evidence at the outset to prevent bias.
    • ›Remove loopholes to avoid shifting goalposts during evaluation.
    • ›Prioritize truth over the need to be right.
  • •Recognizing Limits:
    • ›Clearly acknowledge gaps in knowledge.
    • ›Define the limits of logic applicability.
    • ›Specify conditions where logic holds versus where it breaks down.
B. Stress-Testing Methods
  • •Mirror Test:Represent the opponent’s argument and perspective as impartially and precisely as possible, to fully stress-test their logic.
  • •Graded Confidence:Evaluate the quality of evidence to guide how strongly you hold each belief.
2. SELF-CONTROL & EQUANIMITY
A. Observer Mindset
  • •Non-Identification:Observe thoughts and emotions without identifying with them; separate reasoning from impulses.
B. Effort Allocation
  • •Effort Thresholds:
    • ›Controllable: Invest full effort where actions reliably influence outcomes.
    • ›Partial Control: Scale effort according to realistic level of influence.
    • ›Uncontrollable: Refrain from effort to prevent wasted energy and instability.
C. Decoupling
  • •Internal Anchor:Ground self-image in intentional actions and conscious choices rather than external outcomes.
Principle: Directing effort toward uncontrollable conditions is inefficient and destabilizing.
3. STRESS INOCULATION & RESILIENCE
A. Stress Adaptation Types
  • •Response Profiles:
    • ›Threat: Maintain and defend structure under pressure.
    • ›Tests: Refine methods and responses through feedback.
    • ›Variable Challenges: Apply learned strategies in changing situations.
B. Ideal Conditions
  • •Optimal Stress:Moderate, varied challenges produce the strongest adaptive capacity.
C. Self-Efficacy
  • •Confidence Cycle:Confidence grows through repeated successful adaptation to stress.
Caution: Abandoning agency undermines the ability to adapt and may lead to learned helplessness.
